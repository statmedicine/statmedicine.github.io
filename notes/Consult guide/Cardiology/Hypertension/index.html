Hypertension

Things to check before seeing the patient

  1. What is the BP trend - was this a sudden change?
  2. What medications are they on / have any not been taken?
    • e.g. Hypertension may be due to pain - check if they have had their pain medications
    • have they had antihypertensives witheld?
    • Is anything potentially driving up the BP? (Steroids, OCP, MAOi, stimulants)
  3. Why were they admitted
    1. Is the patient pregnant (i.e. Pre-eclampsia?)
      • immediately call obstetrics registrar if so
    2. Do they have a spinal cord injury? Consider autonomic dysreflexis
      • consider contacting neurosurgery
  4. Are they in a hypertensive emergency?
  5. Are they on antihypertensives?

Approach to the patient

  1. HISTORY
    1. Assess for symptomatic hypertension (i.e. hypertensive emergency)
  2. ASSESSMENT
    1. Airway
    2. Breathing
      • Dyspnoea/crackles on auscultation -> Consider fluid overload/APO
    3. Circulation - take manual BP
      • Consider BPs in both arms if complaining of 'tearing' chest pain (Aortic dissection)
      • Consider ECG and troponins for ?MI
    4. Disability
      • New seizures, confusion -> escalate patient's care, consider CT brain to exclude PRES or ICH
    5. Exposure
    6. Fluids
    7. Glucose
  3. MANAGEMENT
    1. Treat underlying condition where possible
      • Give any missed doses of antihypertensives provided not contraindicated
    2. If no underlying condition visible -> 5-10mg oral amlodipine (if they can tolerate CCB)
      • Consider 25mg GTN patch (if nil aortic stenosis)
        • Ongoing BP assessments q15min for 2 hours or until 3 normotensive measurements - remove GTN patch if SBP < 160mmHg
  4. DOCUMENT

Hypertensive Urgency

Definition: SBP >= 180/110, associated with significant symptoms (headache, dizziness) with mild-moderate nonacute end-organ damage

Management

  1. Give regular BP-lowering drugs if not contra-indicated
  2. Ask registrar before giving new medications
  3. Consider fast-acting BP lowering drugs on low dose, such as
    1. Nifedipine IR 10mg PO (discontinued)
    2. Captopril 12.5mg PO
    3. Cloinidine 100mcg PO
    4. Prazosin 2mg PO
    5. Consider repeat dosage of above after 2 hours
  4. Alternatively, consider a slower-acting BP lowering drug, such as
    • Amlodipine 5mg PO
  5. If patients cannot take oral therapy, can consider IV hydralazine

Note, aim for SBP <= 160mmHg

Hypertensive Emergency

Definition: Severely elevated SBP >= 220/14mmHg, associated with acute end-organ damage or dysfunction, including APO, AKI, hypertensive encephalopathy, papilloedema, ICH

Management

  1. Seek help from senior clinican or ICU specialist - consider arterial line or repeat BP measurements every 5 minutes
  2. Consider IV infusion of a fast-acting short-half life agent
    1. sodium nitroprusside 0.3 microgram/kg/minute by intravenous infusion, increasing by 0.5 microgram/kg/minute every 5 minutes, up to 10 micrograms/kg/minute
      • avoid in CKD, liver impairment and pregnancy
    2. esmolol 500 micrograms/kg intravenously, over 1 minute, followed by 50 to 200 micrograms/kg/minute by intravenous infusion
    3. glyceryl trinitrate 10 micrograms/minute by intravenous infusion, increasing by 5 micrograms/minute every 5 minutes, up to 100 micrograms/minute
    4. labetalol 2 to 4 mg/minute by intravenous infusion until BP is in the target range, followed by 5 to 20 mg/hour (not available in Aus)
    5. phentolamine 1 to 5 mg by intravenous bolus every 5 to 15 minutes until BP is in the target range, followed by 0.5 to 1.0 mg/hour by intravenous infusion (not available in Aus)
  3. Consider an IV bolus if IV infusion not available:
    1. Hydralazine 1mg IV bolus, q1minute up to 5x
    2. Metoprolol tartrate 1mg IV bolus, q1minute up to 5x
    3. Atenolol, Labetalol, Phentolamine
