General notes and advice

Structure

Consults should follow a general structure of ISBAR. Whilst it is not necessary to follow this to the tee, it is a good habit to get into. ISBAR is as follows:

  • Introduction
    • Your name, who you are calling from, what are you calling for
    • Hi, I'm John, the General Medicine RMO. Is this a good time for a consult?
  • Situation
    • The reason you are calling - include pertinent findings, history, exam and radiology results
    • I have a 91 year old lady with new diagnosis of heart failure for which we are requesting your support to initiate antifailure medications on
  • Background
    • The background and presenting complaint of the patient
    • Try your best to figure out the relevant features for the patient and the question being asked
    • She presented to ED 3 days ago in APO. Her formal echo this morning demonstrated an apical akinesis in the LV with a LVEF of 25%. She has no other history of cardiac disease and has had no obvious precipitants including viral or stressors. She has reported 3 months of worsening exertional dyspnoea and her exercise tolerance is limited to 5m on the flat. Her NYHA is probably around IV as she cannot get dressed without feeling breathlessness. She is currently on only perindopril as an antifailure agent. She has a past medical history of uncontrolled T2DM with a HbA1c of 10.5% and an uncontrolled hypertension with SBPs ranging between 180-200 throughout this admission
  • Assessment
    • Here you demonstrate the assessment of the patient. Remember, the person on the other side of the phone has never seen your patient!
    • She examines fluid overloaded and hemodynamically stable. Her blood pressure has been stable at SBP 180 over the last 24h and is otherwise well. She has bilateral pitting oedema to her thighs and bilateral fine crepitations to the mid zones. Her JVP is elevated at 10cm. She is normally on frusemide 20mg OD but we have had to increase this to 40mg IV BD whilst an inpatient and have started a 1.2L fluid restriction.
      • Note here that some of your recommendations/actions are also incorporated into this assessment. Again, this structure of ISBAR does not have to be followed to the tee - you will get more familiar with this as you learn to practice medicine and utilise your knowledge to a greater extent with time.
  • Recommendation
    • You need to now ask your question. Be as specific as possible otherwise you will need to reconsult later
    • This is also a time you can test your own knowledge and ask questions as well
    • Let the registrar know if you are happy for just phone advice or if you wish for a formal consult and for the patient to be seen
    • I'm hoping to get some assistance with firstly uptitrating her antihypertensives and two starting her on more antifailure medications. We're happy for just some phone advice and you do not need to see her.

Common pitfalls

There tend to be recurrent themes in poor consults - there are many driving factors, including high patient load, high occupational demands, etc. However do remember that the registrar you will be consulting to is also going to be very stressed - and the difference between an good and a bad consult can be massive to their workload.

Before consulting, you should do the following:

  • Look through resources such as UpToDate and BMJ BestPractice to make sure you have at least attempted to work up the patient
  • Read through the patients history and notes to see if they've had similar presentations in the past, and what was done about it then
  • See the patient - this is incredibly important as many questions can be answered from just what you recall
    • Remember that the person on the other side of the phone has no clue what your patient looks like
  • Understand the guidelines regarding the issue/question you are asking
  • Know specifically what the question you are asking is, and what question your registrar/consultant wants answered.
  • Know specifically how to discharge the person from the service you are consulting - understand what their requirements are to be followed up, and if the patient is going home with an instituted intervention (E.g. a c-spine collar following a cervical spine fracture), what the follow-up is going to be and how is the patient going to be cleared
