Seizures

Immediate considerations

  1. Risks of ongoing seizures (>5 minutes - status epilepticus)
    • Hypoxia
    • Aspiration
    • Metabolic acidosis (from excessive muscle activity -> lactic acid buildup)
    • Hyperpyrexia (from excessive muscle activity)
    • Cerebral oedema (secondary to hypoxia)
  2. What type of seizure
    • Partial/Focal
    • Generalised tonic-clonc
  3. Has the patient finished seizing?
    • Are they currently conscious?
  4. Was there any concurrent injuries
    • Falling off bed, tongue-bite, incontinence, head injury
  5. Were they admitted for seizures? Head injury? Something that could cause seizures?
  6. Could this be eclampsia?

Approach to the rapid review

A - Airway

  1. Place patient in lateral decubitus
    • Why? To minimise aspiration risk and allow any vomitus to flow out
  2. Look in airway for obstructions
    1. Place in Oropharyngeal/nasopharyngeal airway if trismus present
      • Patient at high risk of occluding upper airway with lax tongue
    2. Yankauer sucker and suction equipment
      • Minimise risk of any aspirations

B - Breathing

  1. Give high-flow O2 (15L/minute) by mask and attach pulse oximetry
    • Why? Seizing patients are high risk of hypoxia and cerebral hypoperfusion

C - Circulation

  1. Ensure HR is taken
  2. Palpate for femoral pulse - presence of a femoral pulse indicates that SBP is AT LEAST 70mmHg

D - Disability

  1. Assess patient's level of consciousness
  2. If patient is at risk of Wenicke's encephalopathy (e.g. alcoholic, malnourished), you must also give Thiamine 300mg IV slow injection

E - Exposure

Probably not as important here, but make sure the patient isn't going to roll into something and get trapped/choke on wires

  1. Drop bed to lowest position
  2. Keep side rails up
  3. Pad patient from rails with rolled up towel or blankets

F - Fluids

  1. Ensure IVC access - 2 large bore IVC in situ
    1. Whilst here, take bloods - BGL, and Basic Bloods
  2. 1000mL IV Normal Saline charted and hydrating patient at XXXmL/hr

G - Glucose

  1. Finger-prick BGLs
    • Hypoglycaemia can be a cause of seizures
  2. Prepare Glucose for low BGL
    • Prepare 50% Dextrose, 50mL bolus if low (up to 100mL bolus MAX)

Prepare for Status Epilepticus

  1. Draw up 10mg Diazepam or 10mg Midazolam in 10mL saline for IV push

Post-seizure management

  1. Order investigations
    • Repeat Basic Bloods and BGLs
    • bHCG to exclude pregnant women and eclampsia
    • Check anticonvulsant levels
    • Blood cultures if sepsis suspected
    • CT Brain non-contrast
      • Look for: Traumatic injury, cause of raised ICP, or infective lesions (e.g. due to immunosuppression)
    • LP (after CT brain) if suspecting intracranial infection or SAH
